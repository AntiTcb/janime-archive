    So this could mean the following things:
    • The movie took place between Episode 80 and before Crash Town.
    • The movie took place after Crash Town and before the WRGP.
    • The movie took place after the WRGP (if we assume the D-Wheel designs can be changed back to the original).

    If we consider the first two options, Paradox has already "been" in the 5D's period (at least in a manner that showed himself to people, like Yusei and co.) and already died in the past by that time. If we consider the last option, the movie's events are yet to happen.

    The thing is, Yusei and co. could still alter things in a way that will affect Paradox's future. It has been said that his identity is yet to be revealed, so we may see him before he ended up in the distant future in whatever form, or Yusei and co. affected the circumstances responsible for his origin. However...

    Paradox claims he came from the distant future, not from "Neo Domino City", for instance. This seems to imply he is a citizen of the society in the distant future. Or, he was sent to the future by some other means and he doesn't know of it. I don't think Paradox built the time machine until the future got destroyed, unless it's as Makoeyes said and he's been experimenting with the future from the beginning and ends up in a variation of his future where the world was destroyed by Duel Monsters. Perhaps it's his meddling which causes a change in the recent future (in Yusei's present) which then causes said destruction and makes humanity evil. Maybe Yusei and co. will encounter him during such an event? Yusei has knowledge of Paradox at the time while Paradox may have not (unless he knows him from, well, history books). He could intercept Paradox and thus altering the events that would've played out, not resulting in the future being destroyed. However, that would create a Grandfather Paradox - if the future didn't get destroyed because of Duel Monsters, Paradox wouldn't have traveled back in time and encounter the protagonists, thus Yusei would've never learned of him, therefore Yusei wouldn't have stopped Paradox in the first place. Unless Paradox is doing something that would have prompted Yusei to intercept him either way, thus not necessarily making it a paradox.

    For how the whole situation works, let me present another example from Chrono Trigger.

    There's a part in the game where you need to bring a stone way back in time in order to power it up, with your goal to reclaim it in the distant future when the power-up is finished. You place the stone in 65 million BC. You travel to 2300 AD, expecting to go to pick the stone up... but wait, it's gone! When you go and explore the present (1000 AD), you discover that someone moved the stone away from its place, making it disappear from the future, altering the future.

    Paradox is the "stone" in this case, which the protagonists encountered in the recent past (traveled back to a recent past period where they still saw the stone intact). But by meeting him in the recent future (move the stone away), they change the distant future (stone is removed in the distant future).

    However, all of this would just make Paradox "a time traveler". Seeing how his identity is going to be revealed in 5D's, it means he's possibly someone important or the writers are just hyping people who wish to know more about Paradox. XD

    Originally Posted by Lia
    Paradox reminds so much of Pegasus.
    Now that you brought it up, I'm tempted to write down this analysis I had in mind - a comparison of Pegasus and Paradox.

    Yes, you're right - Paradox is very much like Pegasus. But at the same time, they are total opposites, thesis and antithesis of each other.

    Let's start with the obvious: Pegasus is the man who created Duel Monsters, the Creator of Duel Monsters. Paradox, wanting to destroy the game, is the Destructor of Duel Monsters.

    They are somewhat opposites in terms of appearance as well. Pegasus has silver hair, while Paradox's hair is blond (or "gold"). Pegasus has/had the Millennium Eye in place of his left eye, while Paradox has the tattoo/eyebrow on the right side of his face. Pegasus is right-handed while Paradox is left-handed.

    Now let's see how they work and what their beliefs are.

    Pegasus works from behind the scenes as long as he could - he worked with the Big Five and the Player Killers rather than fighting Yugi in the open (except for their first duel where he got Yugi to attend Duelist Kingdom, trying to lock him in the "defeat him in an official duel" condition, and their final duel). In other words, he was a man who worked from a distance and behind the scenes while having others do the work for him, unless when he really had to fight himself. Paradox, on the other hand, had himself as the primary piece in his plan (the only one, even, due to being the last human left alive) and acted in the open at all times. You could say he was constantly on the frontline, doing the work himself (unless you count his monsters as his lackeys).

    Pegasus himself never used physical force to deal with his enemies. He always relied on mental combat - manipulation, mind reading and obfuscating stupidity. Paradox uses force - he assaults Yusei for Stardust Dragon, he physically harms Judai and he destroys Domino City, killing Pegasus and all of the civilians.

    I should note that they both used a card to s(t)eal souls: Pegasus had Soul Prison for the Kaibas while Paradox had a blank card for Stardust Dragon (and possibly the other dragons).

    Now, beliefs. Pegasus seems to believe in the mystical, such as magic, entities (eg. God cards), and the Afterlife. Paradox strikes me as a rational man - he treats his entire mission as an "experiment", for example.

    Next, let's look at their decks.

    Both of them heavily rely on a card representing a world - their inner world. Pegasus has Toon World while Paradox has Sin World, supporting their deck themes (Toon and Sin respectively). Toon World transforms regular monsters into Toons, and Sin World "transforms" regular monsters into Sin monsters. Now, Sin World isn't required for the Sin Monsters (as any Field Spell would be fine), but this is Paradox's main Field Spell, and also a main support for his deck, just like Toon World.

    From a symbolical perspective (as we know that the players are reflected in their cards), both cards represent Pegasus and Paradox. Toon World's exterior (a children's book) is Pegasus' playful, childish self, while the inside of the book (especially in the case of the TCG art for Toon World) there are only gloomy buildings, showing Pegasus' true self, the grieving, twisted man. Sin World shows us the vast, infinite space, representing Paradox's world, the world created by sin in which Paradox remained as the last human, all alone.

    Sin World and Toon World are also opposites - Toon World (at least initially) appears as a cheerful, childish world full of inhabitants --the Toons-- which denies death (Toons are immortal and cannot die), and you could see the Toons' immunity to normal monsters' attacks as a way of dodging out of fate's way. Sin World is different: It's an empty world without life, without any happiness, with a feeling of death and the inability to change fate.

    The Toon Monsters and Sin Monsters reflect their owners accordingly. The Toons show Pegasus' childish side with a touch of insanity, while the Sin Monsters follow Paradox's design (the mask and his D-Wheel's seat).

    However, Pegasus' deck runs two themes (Toon and the Illusion monsters) while Paradox runs only one (Sin).

    Another notable aspect is their signature and "Joker card" monsters. Pegasus has Sacrifice and Thousand-Eyes Sacrifice, while Paradox has Sin Paradox Dragon and Sin Truth Dragon. Again, symbolism applies (Pegasus "sacrificing" other people for his goal, while the dragons are a play on Paradox's name and the concept of paradoxes. It is interesting to note that Pegasus' monster got much uglier, while Paradox's monster became... well, more magnificent, perhaps? Both Thousand-Eyes and Sin Truth can be brought out under specific conditions: Sin Truth by Sin Paradigm Shift, Thousand-Eyes by fusion, and both of them involve the respective signature monster. Also, both monsters have fusion involved with them, even though Pegasus is fusing only monsters together while Paradox himself is integrated into Sin Truth.

    And for the last, motives.

    Paradox's motives aren't easy to figure out at this point, as we know little about his identity. We only know his future was destroyed, but we don't know who he is or how his future got destroyed. He wishes to change his hopeless future, which is void of any life at that point. He lost the life he may have wanted to live through (in a good future). We don't know exactly why Paradox desires to save his future, however. Does he want to save everyone? Does he not want to be alone? Does he want to save himself? Does he only want to continue his experiment?

    Pegasus isn't much different - he wants to reunite with Cyndia, perhaps save her from death, even. Like Paradox, he lost his future, his dream of being a famous artist and living with his love forever.

    Both of them walk on a path of tragedy and attempt to reach their goals by ruthless means, only to fall in the end because of their flaws. While this is a "good end" for Pegasus, as he's able to reunite with Cyndia this way, it's eternal loneliness for Paradox - he dies alone, far from his own period, far from anyone, without a body (as he sacrificed it for Sin Truth Dragon), without reaching his goal. But the protagonists still vow to protect the future, implying that by working together, they might protect the future and save it from the destruction Paradox witnessed. How this promise will play out is something we'll find out in the future, I guess.

    And before I forget, both of them have somewhat ironic names. Paradox's name could refer to his goal (destroying Duel Monsters which would result in a Grandfather Paradox), perhaps his origin (him messing up his own future) or just paradoxes, which can be used to describe situations that are ironic. Of course, Paradox's name may be a nickname and not his real name, but we might see the answer in future 5D's episodes.

    Pegasus is named after the mythical horse, yet he also resembles Bellerophon, the man who had slain the chimera with Pegasus' help.

    Originally Posted by Rich's Pegopedia
    The end of Bellerophon's life was most tragic. Two of his children, Laodameia and Isandrus, were slain, the first by Artemis, and the second by Ares. Finally, perhaps driven by the grief for his slain children, Bellerophon mounted Pegasus one last time. Perhaps to challenge Ares and Artemis, perhaps to claim some perceived birthright or earned heroic merit he intently drove straight toward the throne of Zeus: Mount Olympus. Affronted by such conceit, Zeus formed the gadfly, Brize, and sent it to sting Pegasus under the tail, causing him to rear and sending his rider tumbling to the earth. Odious to all Immortals, Bellerophon wandered the earth, his heart consumed with misery, alone, fleeing the haunts of men.
    Pegasus (at least in the anime) wanted to take control of the God cards himself, for which he was punished by the deities themselves, resulting in the man leaving for Egypt to seal them away. At the same time, he was the only one to reach the Gods, as everyone else perished in the expedition.

    In the end, one could easily sum up the two with Paradox's mask: a twisted, destructive side and a sad, lonely person who carries grief on their shoulders.
